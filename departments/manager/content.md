# PART 2: HIGH OUTPUT MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORK

Based on Andy Grove's Intel playbook. The principles that built one of the world's most successful companies.

## 2.1 The Breakfast Factory - Production Thinking

**Core Insight:** Management is production. Think of your team as a factory producing outputs.

### Three Types of Operations

| Operation Type | Definition | Example |
|----------------|------------|---------|
| **Process** | Physical/chemical transformation of materials | Writing code, designing screens |
| **Assembly** | Combining components into a whole | Integrating features, building releases |
| **Test** | Verifying quality meets standards | Code review, QA, user testing |

### The Limiting Step

Every production flow has a **limiting step** - the longest/hardest step that gates everything else.

**How to Identify:**
1. Map your entire workflow end-to-end
2. Measure time at each step
3. The bottleneck IS your production capacity
4. Optimize the bottleneck first (everything else is waste)

**Application:**
- If design is the bottleneck, more developers won't help
- If code review is the bottleneck, faster coding won't help
- Always attack the constraint, not the symptom

### Adding Value at Lowest Cost

**Principle:** Detect problems at the lowest-value stage possible.

| Stage | Value Added | Cost of Defect |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| Requirements | $1 | $1 to fix |
| Design | $10 | $10 to fix |
| Development | $100 | $100 to fix |
| Testing | $500 | $500 to fix |
| Production | $1000+ | $1000+ to fix |

**Implication:** Front-load quality. Invest in requirements and design reviews.

## 2.2 Managerial Leverage

**Definition:** The output generated per unit of managerial activity.

```
Manager's Output = Output of their organization + Output of neighboring organizations they influence
```

### The Leverage Equation

```
Activity Impact = (Leverage) × (Number of people affected) × (Duration of effect)
```

**High Leverage Activities:**
| Activity | Why High Leverage |
|----------|-------------------|
| Training | 1 hour → years of improved performance across many people |
| Setting direction | 1 decision → months of aligned effort |
| Hiring | 1 good hire → years of output |
| Process improvement | 1 change → permanent efficiency gain |
| Knowledge sharing | 1 document → infinite reuse |

**Low Leverage Activities:**
| Activity | Why Low Leverage |
|----------|------------------|
| Doing the work yourself | 1:1 effort to output |
| Attending FYI meetings | No decisions, no learning |
| Reviewing work that's already shipped | Too late to change |
| Micromanaging | Negative leverage (demotivates) |

**Negative Leverage:**
Meddling, waffling, and micromanagement have NEGATIVE leverage - they reduce output below what it would be without you.

**How I Apply This:**
1. Before any action: "Will this multiply output?"
2. Prioritize systemic improvements over one-off fixes
3. Look for patterns that can be reused
4. Design for the 80/20 - what 20% of effort yields 80% of results?

## 2.3 Meetings as Management Medium

**Grove's Principle:** Meetings are the medium of managerial work. A manager who doesn't know how to run meetings cannot be effective.

### Meeting Types

#### 1. One-on-Ones (1:1s)

**Purpose:** The SUBORDINATE's meeting for their issues, concerns, and information sharing.

| Element | Guideline |
|---------|-----------|
| Frequency | Weekly for new/changing roles; bi-weekly for experienced/stable |
| Duration | 30-60 minutes minimum (60 preferred) |
| Owner | The report, not the manager |
| Location | Report's preferred space, not manager's office |

**Agenda Structure:**
1. **Their topics first** (70% of time)
   - What's on your mind?
   - What's blocking you?
   - What do you need from me?
2. **Manager topics** (20% of time)
   - Feedback
   - Information sharing
   - Course corrections
3. **Career/Development** (10% of time)
   - Growth conversations
   - Skill building

**Manager's Role in 1:1s:**
- Listen more than talk (aim for 70/30 split)
- Ask questions, don't give answers immediately
- Take notes and follow up on commitments
- Watch for what's NOT being said

**Warning Signs in 1:1s:**
- Report has nothing to discuss → They're checked out or scared
- Only surface-level topics → Trust isn't there yet
- Same issues recurring → You're not solving problems
- Cancellations → They don't find value (your fault)

#### 2. Staff Meetings

**Purpose:** Peer-level information exchange and decision-making.

| Element | Guideline |
|---------|-----------|
| Frequency | Weekly |
| Duration | 60-90 minutes |
| Attendees | Direct reports only (peers) |
| Manager Role | Facilitator, not presenter |

**Structure:**
1. **Operational review** (15 min) - What's the status?
2. **Information sharing** (15 min) - What does everyone need to know?
3. **Discussion items** (30-45 min) - Decisions and problem-solving
4. **Open floor** (15 min) - Anything else?

**Ground Rules:**
- No surprises (agenda published 24hrs ahead)
- Peers talk to peers (not through manager)
- Decisions made in the room stick
- Disagree and commit

#### 3. Operation Reviews

**Purpose:** Cross-functional alignment on indicators and forecasts.

| Element | Guideline |
|---------|-----------|
| Frequency | Weekly or bi-weekly |
| Duration | 60-120 minutes |
| Attendees | All levels who need alignment |
| Focus | Indicators, not opinions |

**Structure:**
1. Review key indicators vs. targets
2. Identify variances (where are we off-track?)
3. Root cause analysis on variances
4. Action items with owners and deadlines
5. Forecast next period

#### 4. Decision Meetings (Mission-Oriented)

**Purpose:** Make ONE specific decision.

**When to Call:**
- Decision affects multiple teams
- Disagreement exists and won't resolve naturally
- Deadline is approaching

**Structure:**
1. **Frame the decision** (5 min) - What exactly are we deciding?
2. **Present options** (10 min) - Max 3 options with tradeoffs
3. **Discussion** (15 min) - Clarifying questions, concerns
4. **Decision** (5 min) - Who decides? Make the call.
5. **Commitment** (5 min) - Everyone commits, even dissenters

**Who Decides:**
- Whoever has the knowledge AND will live with consequences
- If unclear, the most senior person decides
- Never design by committee

### Meeting Hygiene

**Before:**
- Purpose stated (why are we meeting?)
- Agenda published (what will we cover?)
- Pre-read sent (what should people know?)
- Right people invited (who needs to be there?)

**During:**
- Start on time (respect everyone's schedule)
- Stay on agenda (parking lot for tangents)
- Capture decisions and action items
- End with next steps

**After:**
- Notes sent within 24 hours
- Action items tracked
- Follow-up scheduled if needed

## 2.4 Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)

**Core Principle:** There is no single "right" management style. The right style depends on the TASK-RELEVANT MATURITY of the individual.

### TRM Definition

Task-Relevant Maturity = experience + education + training **for a specific task**

**Key Insight:** Someone can have HIGH TRM for one task and LOW TRM for another. Adjust your style per task, not per person.

### The TRM Model

| TRM Level | Employee State | Manager Style | Communication |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Low** | New to task, uncertain, needs guidance | **Structured** - Tell what, when, how | Task-focused, detailed, frequent check-ins |
| **Medium** | Growing competence, some confidence | **Coaching** - Two-way dialogue | Individual-focused, support + feedback |
| **High** | Expert at task, fully capable | **Delegating** - Set objectives only | Minimal involvement, establish objectives |

### Low TRM Management

**When:** New hire, new project type, new technology, new role

**How to Manage:**
- Provide detailed instructions (what, when, how)
- Check in frequently (daily or multiple times daily)
- Review work before completion, not after
- Give immediate feedback
- This is NOT micromanagement - it's appropriate support

**Signs You're Getting It Right:**
- Employee isn't lost or floundering
- Errors caught early, not after completion
- Confidence grows over time
- TRM increases

**Signs You're Getting It Wrong:**
- Employee is frustrated ("I know what I'm doing!")
- You're still doing this after 3+ months with no change
- Work quality is consistently good but you're still hovering

### Medium TRM Management

**When:** Growing in role, handling some independently, still learning

**How to Manage:**
- Discuss the "what" together, let them decide "how"
- Weekly check-ins, not daily
- Review work at milestones, not constantly
- Coach through problems rather than solving for them
- Give feedback that builds, not just corrects

**The Coaching Conversation:**
1. What are you trying to accomplish?
2. What have you tried so far?
3. What options are you considering?
4. What do you think you should do?
5. How can I help?

### High TRM Management

**When:** Expert at the specific task, proven track record

**How to Manage:**
- Set objectives (the "what")
- Get out of the way
- Provide air cover and resources
- Check in for alignment, not progress
- Trust the outcome

**Warning:** Don't confuse high TRM in one area with high TRM everywhere. A senior engineer may have LOW TRM for:
- People management
- A new programming language
- Working with a new team
- Customer-facing work

### Adjusting Over Time

```
Low TRM → Medium TRM → High TRM
   ↓           ↓           ↓
More structure → Coaching → Delegation
More check-ins → Regular syncs → Light touch
Task-focused → Individual-focused → Objective-focused
```

**Critical:** TRM can DROP when:
- New responsibilities added
- Context changes (new team, new project)
- Extended break or absence
- Burnout or personal issues

When TRM drops, temporarily increase structure. This isn't a demotion - it's appropriate support.

## 2.5 Dual Reporting & Hybrid Organizations

### Why Matrix Organizations Exist

**The Tension:**
- Functional groups (engineering, design, product) provide expertise and consistency
- Mission groups (product teams, projects) provide focus and speed
- You can't have both at maximum simultaneously

**The Solution:** Matrix/dual reporting where someone reports to both.

### Types of Dual Reporting

| Structure | Primary Report | Secondary Report | Use When |
|-----------|---------------|------------------|----------|
| **Strong Functional** | Functional manager | Project lead | Expertise matters most |
| **Strong Project** | Project manager | Functional advisor | Delivery matters most |
| **Balanced Matrix** | Both equally | Both equally | Both matter (hardest to do well) |

### Making Dual Reporting Work

**For the Person with Two Bosses:**
1. Clarify whose priority wins in conflicts
2. Keep both bosses informed (no surprises)
3. Escalate conflicts quickly, don't try to resolve alone
4. Get alignment on performance criteria

**For Managers in Matrix:**
1. Communicate frequently with co-manager
2. Never put employee in the middle of disagreements
3. Align on expectations upfront
4. Single voice on performance

### Grove's Hybrid Model

**Principle:** Most organizations should be hybrids - neither pure functional nor pure mission-oriented.

**Key Questions to Determine Structure:**
1. What changes more often - the technology or the market?
2. Where does knowledge need to accumulate?
3. Where does speed matter most?

## 2.6 Performance Reviews

### Purpose (What Reviews Are FOR)

**Primary:** Improve the subordinate's performance.

**NOT:**
- Not primarily for compensation decisions
- Not for ranking employees
- Not for documentation (though it helps)
- Not for surprises

### No Surprises Rule

**Principle:** Nothing in a performance review should be new information.

If feedback was never given during the year, it CANNOT appear in the review.

### Delivering Performance Reviews

**The L3 Approach:**
1. **Level 1:** What they did well (specific examples)
2. **Level 2:** What they need to improve (specific examples)
3. **Level 3:** How to improve (actionable guidance)

**Structure:**
1. Start with Level 1 (acknowledge strengths)
2. Transition: "Here's where you can grow..."
3. Present Level 2 with Level 3 for each point
4. End with forward-looking development plan

### Handling Poor Performance

**Three Stages:**

| Stage | Duration | Action |
|-------|----------|--------|
| **Ignore and Hope** | 0 days | NEVER DO THIS |
| **Formal Warning** | 30-60 days | Clear expectations, check-ins, documentation |
| **Exit** | When improvement isn't happening | Part ways cleanly |

**The Conversation for Poor Performers:**
1. State the gap clearly (here's the expectation, here's reality)
2. Ask for their perspective (is there something I don't know?)
3. Commit to support (how can I help you succeed?)
4. Set clear timeline (you have X weeks to show improvement)
5. Define what improvement looks like (measurable)
6. Document everything

### The Blast Radius of Keeping Poor Performers

Keeping a poor performer hurts:
- **Them** - They're failing and know it
- **The team** - Others pick up slack, resent it
- **You** - Your credibility suffers
- **The org** - Standards erode

**Rule:** If after genuine coaching and support, someone isn't improving in 90 days, they won't. Make the hard call.

## 2.7 Training as High Leverage

**Grove's Assertion:** Training is the highest-leverage activity a manager can do.

### The Math

If you spend 4 hours preparing and delivering training that improves 10 people's performance by 1% over the next year...

```
10 people × 2000 hours/year × 1% improvement = 200 hours of improved output
Your investment = 4 hours
Leverage = 50x
```

### Manager's Training Responsibilities

**Direct Training:**
- Teach what you know
- Share institutional knowledge
- Conduct working sessions
- Review work as a teaching moment

**Training Infrastructure:**
- Ensure onboarding exists and works
- Create documentation for repeated processes
- Build feedback loops into learning
- Identify and develop training capability in others

### Knowledge Transfer Patterns

**Effective:**
- Paired work (senior + junior)
- Working sessions with real problems
- Documentation written by learner, reviewed by expert
- Post-mortems and retrospectives

**Ineffective:**
- "Read the docs" with no support
- Tribal knowledge that stays in heads
- Training once with no reinforcement
- Experts too busy to teach

## 2.8 Motivation Framework

### Two Types of Motivation (Grove's Model)

| Type | Driver | Manager's Role |
|------|--------|----------------|
| **Competence-Driven** | Mastering the work itself | Provide challenging work, feedback, growth |
| **Achievement-Driven** | Accomplishing goals, winning | Set clear goals, measure results, celebrate wins |

### The Self-Actualization Frame

**Insight:** Most knowledge workers are already past survival needs. They're seeking:
- Mastery (getting better at what they do)
- Autonomy (control over how they work)
- Purpose (connection to why it matters)

**Manager's Job:** Create conditions where self-actualization is possible.

### Fear as Motivator

**Grove's Nuance:** Fear of failure IS a motivator, but:
- Fear of external punishment = compliance, not performance
- Fear of not meeting own standards = internal drive
- Fear of letting team down = healthy pressure

**Application:** Set high expectations. Let people hold themselves accountable. Don't threaten.

## 2.9 OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

Every task gets decomposed into OKRs before work begins.

### Objectives (The "What")
- What are we trying to achieve?
- Why does this matter?
- What does success look like?

**Good Objectives Are:**
- Qualitative and inspirational
- Time-bound (quarter or project)
- Aggressive but achievable (60-70% confidence)
- Memorable (you can say them without notes)

### Key Results (The "How We Know")
- How do we know we succeeded?
- What are the concrete deliverables?
- What metrics define "done"?

**Good Key Results Are:**
- Quantitative and measurable
- Specific numbers, not ranges
- Outcomes, not activities
- Stretch goals (if you hit 100%, they weren't ambitious enough)

**Example OKR for "Build a pricing page":**

**Objective:** Create a pricing page that maximizes conversions while clearly communicating value

**Key Results:**
1. Pricing tiers clearly differentiate value (hormozi check)
2. Visual hierarchy guides eyes to recommended tier (design check)
3. CTAs have clear affordances and reduce friction (design check)
4. Page loads in <2s and is mobile-responsive (developer-hat check)
5. All three domains integrate cohesively (manager check)

## 2.10 Process Thinking

Projects are production lines with defined phases.

```
INPUTS → TRANSFORMATION → QUALITY CONTROL → OUTPUT
```

**Phase 1: Inputs**
- User requirements (explicit)
- Implicit requirements (what wasn't said but matters)
- Context (existing codebase, brand, constraints)
- Success criteria (OKRs)

**Phase 2: Transformation**
- Skill orchestration (which skills, what sequence)
- Domain work (each skill applies its frameworks)
- Integration (combining outputs)

**Phase 3: Quality Control**
- Gate checks (did each skill meet its standards?)
- Integration check (do the pieces fit?)
- OKR verification (did we hit our key results?)

**Phase 4: Output**
- Polished deliverable
- Documentation (if needed)
- Handoff (if needed)

## 2.11 Indicator-Based Decisions

I measure. I don't guess.

### Types of Indicators

| Indicator Type | Examples | Used For |
|----------------|----------|----------|
| **Output Indicators** | Lines of code, screens designed, features shipped | Measuring productivity |
| **Quality Indicators** | Bug count, usability scores, conversion rates | Measuring excellence |
| **Leading Indicators** | Code review feedback, early user testing | Predicting future issues |
| **Lagging Indicators** | Customer complaints, refund rates | Confirming past decisions |

### Pairing Indicators

**Principle:** Every output indicator needs a quality indicator pair.

| Output Only | Problem | With Quality Pair |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| Lines of code | Bloat, complexity | + Bug rate per 1000 lines |
| Features shipped | Unused features | + Feature adoption rate |
| Tickets closed | Cherry-picking easy ones | + Cycle time + Customer satisfaction |

### How I Use Indicators

1. Define success metrics before starting
2. Check indicators at each phase
3. Adjust approach based on indicator signals
4. Never ship without indicator validation

### The Black Box Test

**Can you answer these questions for your team?**
1. What is our current output rate?
2. What is our current quality level?
3. Are we getting better or worse?
4. What is our biggest constraint?

If you can't answer these from data, you're managing blind.

# PART 7: ANTI-PATTERNS

Mohammad, these are the patterns I'll push back on - not to be difficult, but because I've seen them hurt projects. I've got your back, so I won't let these slip through.

## 7.1 Partial Solutions

**What it looks like:** "Here's most of it, we can add the rest later"

**Why I'll push back:** Later never comes. You deserve to ship complete work.

**How we fix it:** Break into smaller complete pieces if needed, but each piece ships whole.

## 7.2 "It Technically Works"

**What it looks like:** Functionality exists but quality is poor

**Why I'll push back:** Working isn't the bar. Excellence is the bar - and you can hit it.

**How we fix it:** Apply quality gates. Meet the 8+ threshold.

## 7.3 One Domain Overriding Others

**What it looks like:** "The code is clean but the UX is bad" or "The design is great but the offer is weak"

**Why I'll push back:** All domains must pass. Excellence in one doesn't excuse gaps in others.

**How we fix it:** Rebalance. We don't ship until all domains score 8+.

## 7.4 Missing Implicit Requirements

**What it looks like:** "You didn't ask for that"

**Why I'll push back:** Implicit requirements are still requirements. Anticipation is part of building great products.

**How we fix it:** Think like the end user. What would they expect that wasn't said?

## 7.5 Disconnected Pieces

**What it looks like:** Great components that don't fit together

**Why I'll push back:** Integration is not optional. The whole must work, not just the parts.

**How we fix it:** Check integration gate. Ensure cohesive end-to-end experience.

## 7.6 Premature Shipping

**What it looks like:** "Let's ship and iterate"

**Why I'll push back:** First impressions matter. Your users don't give second chances.

**How we fix it:** Ship when it's 8+. Not before.

## 7.7 Scope Creep Without Acknowledgment

**What it looks like:** Requirements grew but timeline/effort didn't adjust

**Why I'll push back:** Expanded scope with same resources = quality drops. You deserve better.

**How we fix it:** Re-scope, re-plan, or explicitly accept tradeoffs together.

## 7.8 Copy-Paste Without Understanding

**What it looks like:** Code/design copied from elsewhere without adaptation

**Why I'll push back:** Context matters. What works elsewhere may not work here.

**How we fix it:** Understand, then adapt. Never blind copy.

# PART 9: THE MANAGER'S PATH - CAREER LADDER & LEADERSHIP GROWTH

## 9.1 Mentorship vs Sponsorship

**These are different. Know the difference.**

### Mentorship

**Definition:** Giving advice, guidance, and wisdom based on experience.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Focus | Teaching and advising |
| Effort | Lower - share knowledge when asked |
| Risk | Low - you give advice, they decide |
| Power dynamic | Can happen at any level |

**Good Mentorship:**
- Listen first, advise second
- Share experiences, not prescriptions
- Connect to resources and people
- Be available, not overbearing
- Let them make their own decisions

**Mentor Questions:**
- "What have you tried?"
- "What do you think you should do?"
- "Here's what worked for me in a similar situation..."
- "Have you considered X?"

### Sponsorship

**Definition:** Using your political capital to advocate for someone's advancement.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Focus | Opportunity and advancement |
| Effort | Higher - active advocacy |
| Risk | Your reputation tied to their success |
| Power dynamic | Requires seniority/influence |

**Sponsorship Actions:**
- Recommend them for high-visibility projects
- Speak up for them in promotion discussions
- Put their name forward for stretch assignments
- Introduce them to influential people
- Defend them when they're criticized unfairly

**Critical Insight:** Underrepresented groups often get mentorship when they need sponsorship. Mentorship feels good but doesn't advance careers. Sponsorship does.

### When to Provide Each

| Their Need | Provide |
|------------|---------|
| "I don't know how to approach this problem" | Mentorship |
| "I want to get promoted but don't know what's missing" | Mentorship |
| "I've done great work but no one notices" | Sponsorship |
| "I want to lead this big initiative" | Sponsorship |
| "I want to grow my skills" | Mentorship |
| "I'm ready for more responsibility" | Sponsorship |

## 9.3 Managing People (New Manager)

**The hardest transition in tech: from doing the work to enabling others.**

### The First 1:1

**Goals:** Build relationship, understand context, set expectations.

**Questions to Ask:**
1. How do you like to receive feedback?
2. What's your preferred communication style?
3. What's going well that I should know about?

**Set Expectations:**
- Here's how I'll work with you
- Here's what I expect from you
- Here's how we'll handle disagreements
- Here's how to reach me urgently

### Feedback Framework

**Timing:**
- Positive feedback: Public OR private, but soon
- Constructive feedback: ALWAYS private, ALWAYS soon
- Never wait for performance review to give feedback

**The SBI Model (Situation-Behavior-Impact):**
1. **Situation:** "In yesterday's meeting..."
2. **Behavior:** "...when you interrupted Sarah..."
3. **Impact:** "...it made her hesitant to share ideas."

**Then:** Ask for their perspective, discuss how to improve.

### Delegation Framework

| Task Type | Delegate To | How Much Guidance |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Routine, low-risk | Anyone capable | Minimal |
| Routine, higher-risk | Experienced | Light oversight |
| Stretch assignment | High-potential | More support |
| Critical path | Most reliable | Clear checkpoints |
| Nobody can do it | ... | You do it (temporarily) |

**Delegation Levels:**
1. Do exactly as I say
2. Research and recommend (I decide)
3. Research, recommend, and act (but tell me first)
4. Act and tell me after
5. Act, no need to tell me

Match delegation level to task-relevant maturity.

### New Manager Mistakes

|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Trying to be liked** | Conflict avoidance | Your job is respect, not popularity |
| **Doing the work yourself** | Faster short-term | Teach, don't take over |
| **Micromanaging** | Anxiety about quality | Set checkpoints, not surveillance |
| **Avoiding hard conversations** | Discomfort | Delay makes it worse - address early |
| **Playing favorites** | Unconscious bias | Distribute opportunities fairly |
| **No 1:1s** | "I'm too busy" | This IS the job |

### Managing Former Peers

**The Challenge:** Yesterday you were equals. Today you're their boss.

**Principles:**
1. Address it directly: "This is weird, I know. Let's talk about it."
2. Treat everyone fairly - overcorrect for the temptation to favor old friends
3. Don't apologize for decisions - own your new role
4. Expect some relationships to change - that's okay
5. Those who can't adjust may need to move on

## 9.4 Managing a Team

### Staying Technical vs. Delegating

**The Question:** How much should I still code?

**Answer Grid:**

| Team Size | Stay Technical By | Typical Coding |
|-----------|------------------|----------------|
| 2-3 | Being a player-coach | 50-70% |
| 4-6 | Debugging, code review, small features | 30-50% |
| 7-10 | Code review, prototyping | 10-30% |
| 10+ | Architecture decisions, no regular coding | 0-10% |

**Warning Signs You've Gone Too Far:**
- You're the bottleneck on code reviews
- You're not in important conversations
- You don't know what's happening on your team
- Your direct reports are struggling without support

### Hiring for Your Team

**The Hiring Loop:**
1. Define the role (not a clone of who left)
2. Sell the opportunity (why would someone want this?)
3. Source candidates (referrals, recruiters, inbound)
4. Screen (phone, take-home, on-site)
5. Decide (quickly - good candidates don't wait)
6. Close (make the offer compelling)

**Interview Principles:**
- Test for the job, not general intelligence
- Structured interviews beat unstructured
- Multiple perspectives reduce bias
- Weak hire costs more than unfilled role
- Diversity requires intentional effort

**Manager's Role in Hiring:**
- Define success criteria before interviews
- Calibrate interviewers
- Make the call when split decisions
- Close candidates personally
- Own onboarding success

### Creating a Healthy Team Culture

**Signals of Health:**
- People speak up in meetings
- Disagreements happen openly, not behind backs
- Information flows freely
- Mistakes are learning opportunities
- People help each other without being asked

**Signals of Problems:**
- Silence in meetings (fear)
- Gossip and side conversations
- Blame culture
- Information hoarding
- Cliques and factions

**Your Levers:**
- Model the behavior you want
- Reward good behaviors publicly
- Address bad behaviors quickly
- Hire for culture contribution
- Remove persistent problems

### Managing Your Time

**The Calendar Audit:**
1. Categorize last 2 weeks of meetings
2. Calculate: 1:1s, team meetings, cross-functional, reactive, focus time
3. Identify what's missing (usually: focus time, strategic thinking)
4. Protect what matters (block calendar)
5. Reduce or delegate what doesn't

**Time Allocation Guide (Manager of ICs):**

| Activity | Target % |
|----------|----------|
| 1:1s with reports | 20-25% |
| Team rituals (standups, planning) | 10-15% |
| Cross-functional collaboration | 15-20% |
| Hiring | 10-15% (when actively hiring) |
| Focus/strategic time | 10-20% |
| Reactive/unplanned | 10-20% |

### Shield vs Expose

**The Question:** How much organizational noise should you pass to your team?

**Shield (Absorb the Noise):**
- Distracting organizational drama
- Pressure that won't help them perform
- Context that's not actionable
- Politics above their level

**Expose (Share Transparently):**
- Context that helps them prioritize
- Changes that affect them
- Business realities they need to know
- Information that shows trust

**Principle:** Shield from noise, expose to context.

## 9.5 Managing Multiple Teams

### Tech Lead of Tech Leads

**New Challenges:**
- You can't be in every conversation
- Technical decisions happen without you
- Different teams have different cultures
- Alignment is harder

**Your New Job:**
- Set technical direction across teams
- Ensure consistency where it matters
- Allow autonomy where it doesn't
- Resolve cross-team conflicts
- Develop your tech leads

### Time Management Across Teams

**Splitting Attention:**
| Approach | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|
| Equal time per team | Teams are similar in maturity and need |
| Weighted by need | One team is struggling or ramping |
| Rotating focus | Quarterly deep-dives per team |
| Crisis mode | One team needs all attention (temporarily) |

**Anti-pattern:** Spending all time on struggling team while strong teams drift.

### Priority and Resource Juggling

**When Teams Compete for Resources:**
1. Make the tradeoff explicit (not hidden)
2. Connect to business priorities
3. Commit to a decision (no waffling)
4. Revisit when circumstances change

**Cross-Team Dependency Management:**
- Identify dependencies early
- Don't let one team block another indefinitely
- Build interfaces, not integrations (reduce coupling)
- Cross-team tech leads should talk directly

### Standardization vs Autonomy

| Standardize When | Allow Autonomy When |
|------------------|---------------------|
| Security, compliance | Implementation details |
| Core infrastructure | Team process |
| Key interfaces/APIs | Internal architecture |
| On-call standards | Feature prioritization |
| Hiring process | Team rituals |

**Principle:** Standardize for leverage, allow autonomy for ownership.

## 9.6 Managing Managers

**The leap:** From leading makers to leading leaders.

### Skip-Level 1:1s

**Purpose:** See around corners, build relationships, gather unfiltered info.

**Frequency:** Monthly or quarterly (not as frequent as direct 1:1s).

**Questions:**
- How's your manager doing? What could they do better?
- What's frustrating about working here?
- What would you do if you were in my shoes?
- What should I know that I probably don't?
- How are you doing on your career growth?

**Critical:** Don't undermine the manager. Route feedback through proper channels.

### Debugging Dysfunctional Teams

**Symptoms to Watch:**
- High turnover
- Consistent complaints in skip-levels
- Missed deadlines
- Low morale
- Manager making excuses

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Is the manager providing direction and clarity?
2. Are 1:1s happening and effective?
3. Is feedback being given?
4. Are problems addressed or avoided?
5. Does the team trust their manager?

**Interventions:**
| Issue | Intervention |
|-------|-------------|
| Skill gap | Coach and train |
| Will gap | Frank conversation, then exit if no change |
| Context gap | Provide more support and information |
| Resource gap | Adjust expectations or add resources |
| Wrong role | Move them to right role |

### Coaching Managers Through Problems

**Your role:** Ask questions, don't solve for them.

**The Coaching Flow:**
1. What's the situation? (Let them describe fully)
2. What have you tried? (Understand their approach)
3. What options are you considering? (Expand their thinking)
4. What do you think you should do? (Draw out their judgment)
5. What do you need from me? (Provide targeted support)

**When to Step In vs Coach:**
| Step In | Coach |
|---------|-------|
| Urgent, high-stakes | There's time to learn |
| They've tried everything | There's more to try |
| It's your mess to clean up | It's theirs to own |
| They're in over their head | They can grow into it |

### When to Fire a Manager

**Warning:** This is one of the hardest decisions. Firing a manager affects a whole team.

**Reasons to Fire:**
- Loss of team's trust (unrecoverable)
- Ethical violations
- Repeated failure to act on clear feedback
- Toxicity that spreads
- Fundamentally wrong for the role

**Before Firing:**
1. Ensure expectations were clear
2. Provide direct feedback and time to improve
3. Consider if another role is appropriate
4. Get HR/legal alignment
5. Plan for the team's transition

**The Conversation:**
- Direct and clear (no "transition" language)
- Compassionate (this is hard for them too)
- Focus on role fit, not personal failure
- Have next steps ready

## 9.8 Culture Creation

### Culture Is What You Do, Not What You Say

**This Matters:** Your stated values mean nothing. What you reward, punish, and tolerate IS your culture.

**Examples:**

| You Say | But If You Do This | Your Actual Culture |
|---------|-------------------|---------------------|
| "We value work-life balance" | Promote people who work 70 hours | Overwork is rewarded |
| "We value feedback" | Never give hard feedback | Avoidance |
| "We value excellence" | Ship half-finished features | "Good enough" is fine |
| "We value diversity" | Only hire from 2 schools | Homogeneous |

### Values in Action

**To Create a Value:**
1. Define it concretely (not "integrity" but "we keep our commitments")
2. Model it personally
3. Reward it publicly
4. Address violations consistently
5. Hire for it

**Test Your Values:**
- Does this value cause painful tradeoffs?
- Have you ever NOT done something because of this value?
- Would you fire someone for violating this value?
- If yes to all: it's a real value

### Changing Culture

**Hard Truth:** Culture change is slow, painful, and often fails.

**What Works:**
1. Change behavior first, beliefs follow
2. New people bring new norms
3. Consistent enforcement over time
4. Leaders model the change
5. Stories of the new culture

**What Doesn't Work:**
- Announcing values without action
- Expecting overnight change
- Keeping culture carriers who resist
- Inconsistent enforcement

**Timeline:** Culture change takes 2-5 years minimum.

# PART 10: AN ELEGANT PUZZLE - SYSTEMS OF ENGINEERING MANAGEMENT

Based on Will Larson's systems thinking approach to engineering management. Treat organizations as complex systems.

## 10.1 Organizations as Systems

**Core Insight:** Organizations are complex systems with feedback loops, emergent behavior, and unintended consequences. Optimize for the system, not individual components.

### Systems Thinking Principles

**1. Second-Order Effects**
Every change creates downstream effects. Think two steps ahead.

| Action | First-Order Effect | Second-Order Effect |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Add more engineers | More capacity | More coordination overhead |
| Mandate code reviews | Better quality | Slower velocity |
| Performance bonuses | Individual effort | Less collaboration |
| Open offices | More communication | More interruption |

**2. Feedback Loops**
Systems self-regulate through feedback. Identify your loops.

| Loop Type | Example | Effect |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| Reinforcing | Good engineers attract good engineers | Amplifies (good or bad) |
| Balancing | Burnout leads to attrition leads to hiring | Self-corrects |
| Delayed | Technical debt accumulates invisibly | Surprises later |

**3. Local vs Global Optimization**
Optimizing one team can hurt the whole organization.

**Example:** Team A hoards the best engineers. Team A ships fast. Teams B, C, D struggle. Organization underperforms.

**Principle:** Ask "What happens if everyone does this?"

### The System Perspective

**Before making a change, ask:**
1. What problem am I solving?
2. What are the second-order effects?
3. What feedback loops am I creating?
4. Does this optimize locally or globally?
5. How will I know if it's working?

## 10.2 The Four States of a Team

**Larson's Core Model:** Every team is in one of four states, and each state requires different interventions.

### State 1: Falling Behind

**Symptoms:**
- Backlog grows faster than it shrinks
- Everything is urgent
- Team is working hard but slipping backward
- Quality is dropping
- People are stressed

**Root Cause:** Work in > Capacity out

**Interventions:**
1. **Add people** (if available)
2. **Reduce scope** (say no to new work)
3. **Eliminate waste** (stop doing low-value work)
4. **Reduce WIP** (focus on fewer things)

**Warning:** Don't just push harder. The team is already maxed.

### State 2: Treading Water

**Symptoms:**
- Can complete current work
- But can't make progress on improvements
- Technical debt accumulates
- No time for innovation
- Feels like a treadmill

**Root Cause:** All capacity absorbed by current demands

**Interventions:**
1. **Reduce WIP** (fewer things at once = faster completion)
2. **Protect improvement time** (20% for debt)
3. **Stabilize team** (no reorgs, no changes)
4. **Create slack** (not everyone at 100%)

**Goal:** Create space for improvement.

### State 3: Repaying Debt

**Symptoms:**
- Making progress on backlog
- Paying down technical debt
- Quality improving
- Some slack for improvement
- Team feels hopeful

**Root Cause:** Capacity > Current demands (temporarily)

**Interventions:**
1. **Protect this state** (don't add scope)
2. **Keep team stable** (no changes)
3. **Add time, not pressure**
4. **Invest in foundations**

**Warning:** This state is fragile. Don't squander it.

### State 4: Innovating

**Symptoms:**
- Debt is paid
- Team is proactively improving
- Taking on stretch work
- Experiments happening
- High morale

**Root Cause:** Healthy capacity with controlled demand

**Interventions:**
1. **Add slack** (buffer against returning to treading water)
2. **Maintain it** (the goal is to stay here)
3. **Protect from scope creep**
4. **Celebrate and share**

### State Transitions

```
Falling Behind → Treading Water → Repaying Debt → Innovating
     ↓               ↓                ↓              ↓
  ADD PEOPLE    REDUCE WIP      ADD TIME      ADD SLACK
  or REDUCE     and STABILIZE   and PROTECT   and MAINTAIN
  SCOPE
```

**Critical:** You can't skip states. A falling-behind team can't innovate. Fix the fundamentals first.

### Diagnosing Your Teams

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Is backlog growing? | Falling behind | Treading water or better |
| Is there time for improvements? | Repaying debt or better | Treading water |
| Is innovation happening? | Innovating | Repaying debt or below |
| Is there slack? | Healthy | Watch for regression |

## 10.3 Team Sizing

### The Magic Numbers

| Metric | Ideal Range | Why |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| Engineers per team | 6-8 | Enough for complex work, small enough for cohesion |
| Reports per manager | 5-7 | Enough 1:1 time per person |
| Teams per manager-of-managers | 3-5 | Enough context per team |
| Minimum for on-call | 8 | Sustainable rotation (no more than 1 in 8 weeks) |

### Small Teams (< 4)

**Problems:**
- Can't cover all skills needed
- One absence hurts badly
- Hard to sustain on-call
- Low diversity of ideas

**When Okay:**
- Highly specialized work
- Temporary incubation
- Senior, experienced team

### Large Teams (> 10)

**Problems:**
- Coordination overhead
- Manager can't give enough time per person
- Information doesn't flow
- Sub-cliques form

**When Okay:**
- Split into sub-teams soon
- Temporary during hiring surge
- Very stable, autonomous work

### Team Design Principles

**1. Mission Alignment**
Every team should have a clear mission. If you can't explain it in a sentence, it's too broad.

**2. Full Cycle Ownership**
Teams should own services end-to-end (build, run, maintain, improve).

**3. Minimize Dependencies**
Teams that depend on other teams move slower. Design for autonomy.

**4. Stable Teams**
Constantly reforming teams lose productivity. Changes should be rare.

## 10.4 Staying on the Path: Migrations

### Why Migrations Fail

**Common Failure Modes:**
1. Started but never finished (80% done forever)
2. Two systems running in parallel (double maintenance)
3. Edge cases never migrated
4. Nobody owns completion

### The Migration Framework

**Phase 1: Commit or Don't**
- Can you actually finish this?
- Do you have sponsorship?
- Is the ROI clear?
- If any no: don't start

**Phase 2: Design for Completion**
- Define "done" precisely
- Identify all edge cases upfront
- Build rollback capability
- Set milestones and checkpoints

**Phase 3: Grow-Shrink-Bridge**

| Pattern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Grow** | New system alongside old |
| **Shrink** | Migrate traffic/data gradually |
| **Bridge** | Maintain compatibility during transition |

**Phase 4: Endgame**
- Assign specific owner to "last 10%"
- Set hard deadline for old system shutdown
- Celebrate completion

### Migration Math

**The True Cost:**

```
Migration Cost = Build New + Migrate Data + Dual-Run Period + Cleanup Old
```

**Most Underestimated:** The dual-run period where you maintain both systems.

## 10.5 Work the Policy, Not the Exceptions

**Core Principle:** Good systems should handle most cases automatically. If you're constantly making exceptions, fix the system.

### The Exception Problem

**Why Exceptions Are Bad:**
1. They're unfair (who gets exceptions?)
2. They don't scale (every exception takes your time)
3. They undermine the policy
4. They create resentment

**Example:**
- Policy: No remote work
- Exceptions: Senior people get remote
- Result: Junior people feel unfairly treated, leave

### Building Better Policies

**Instead of exceptions, build flexibility into the policy:**

| Exception-Based | Policy-Based |
|-----------------|-------------|
| "Let me approve that special request" | "Here are the criteria for approval" |
| "You're an exception because you're senior" | "At this level, you get X" |
| "I'll make it work for you" | "Anyone in this situation gets Y" |

**Policy Design Questions:**
1. What edge cases will this create?
2. Can the policy handle 80% of cases automatically?
3. Is it fair if applied consistently?
4. Does it scale?

### When Exceptions Are Okay

**Rare Cases:**
- True one-time situations
- While you're fixing the policy
- Crisis mode (but fix the policy after)

**Never:**
- Favoritism
- Avoiding difficult conversations
- Kicking the can on policy fixes

## 10.6 The Art of Saying No

**Reality:** You will say no more than yes. Get good at it.

### Why Saying No Is Hard

- We want to be helpful
- We avoid conflict
- We fear political consequences
- We underestimate our own constraints

### The No Framework

**1. Explain Your Constraints**
"I'd love to help, but we're at capacity with X, Y, Z priorities."

**2. Offer Alternatives**
"I can't do that, but here's what I CAN do..."
"We can't do this now, but we could revisit in Q3."

**3. Redirect to Trade-offs**
"If we do this, what should we stop doing?"
"I can do this if you can get me X resources."

**4. Be Clear and Direct**
"No" is better than "maybe" that becomes no later.

### Saying No to Leadership

**When the ask comes from above:**
1. Clarify the ask: "Help me understand what success looks like"
2. Share constraints: "Here's what would need to change for us to do this"
3. Offer alternatives: "Here are options for how to approach this"
4. Commit or escalate: "I can do X but not Y. Should we escalate?"

**Never:** Just say yes and miss the deadline.

## 10.7 Model, Document, Share

**Principle:** Instead of being a hero who solves all problems, create systems that let others solve problems.

### The Hero Trap

**Signs You're in It:**
- You're the only one who can solve certain problems
- Knowledge lives in your head
- You're always in crisis mode
- You can't take vacation

**The Cost:**
- You're a bottleneck
- You can't scale
- You'll burn out
- Team can't grow

### The Alternative: Model, Document, Share

**1. Model**
Do it yourself the first 2-3 times. Show how it's done.

**2. Document**
Write down what you learned. Make it findable.

**3. Share**
Teach others. Let them do it. Get out of the way.

**Example:**

| Hero Approach | Model-Document-Share Approach |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| "I'll handle the outage" | "Let's handle this together, I'll walk you through it" |
| "Just come to me with problems" | "Here's the troubleshooting runbook" |
| "I know how this works" | "Here's the documentation on how this works" |

### Building Organizational Knowledge

**Systems That Work:**
- Decision logs (why we made choices)
- Runbooks (how to handle situations)
- Architecture docs (how things work)
- Onboarding guides (how to get up to speed)

**Keep Them Updated:**
- Assign owners
- Review quarterly
- Update after every incident
- Link from relevant contexts

## 10.8 Managing Technical Quality

**Larson's Insight:** Quality is a system property, not an individual choice.

### The Quality System

**Quality = (Skills × Process × Time × Incentives) / Pressure**

| Factor | Impact on Quality |
|--------|-------------------|
| Skills | Can they do it right? |
| Process | Does the process enable quality? |
| Time | Is there time to do it right? |
| Incentives | Is quality rewarded? |
| Pressure | Is speed overriding quality? |

### Hot Spots and Cold Spots

**Hot Spots:** Areas with frequent changes, high coupling, low test coverage.
- High risk of quality issues
- Focus investment here

**Cold Spots:** Stable areas, low change frequency, well-tested.
- Low risk
- Don't over-invest

**Where to Invest:**
```
Highest Priority: Hot spot + High business impact
Medium Priority: Hot spot + Low impact OR Cold spot + High impact
Lower Priority: Cold spot + Low impact
```

### Quality Investment Types

| Type | When to Use | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Pay Down Debt** | Team is repaying debt state | Refactoring, test coverage |
| **Quality Improvements** | Team has slack | Better tooling, automation |
| **Quality Gates** | Problems recurring | Code review, CI/CD checks |
| **Training** | Skill gaps | Workshops, pairing |

### Technical Quality Team

**When to Create One:**
- Quality is systematically low
- Needs cross-team solutions
- Platform/infrastructure improvements

**What They Do:**
- Build shared tools
- Establish standards
- Provide guidance
- Raise quality floors

**What They Don't Do:**
- Own other teams' quality
- Fix all problems themselves
- Become a bottleneck

## 10.9 Close Out / Endgame

**Larson's Key Insight:** Starting projects is easy. Finishing them is a skill.

### The Last 10% Problem

**Why Projects Stall at 90%:**
- The easy parts are done
- The hard parts remain
- New shiny things distract
- Nobody owns completion

**The Math:**
The last 10% often takes 50% of the total effort.

### Endgame Strategies

**1. Assign a Closer**
Name ONE person responsible for getting to done.

**2. Scope Hammer**
Aggressively cut scope to finish.
- "What's the minimum for launch?"
- "What can wait for v1.1?"
- "What can we cut entirely?"

**3. Time-Box**
Set a hard deadline. Work backward.

**4. War Room**
Dedicate people and time to finishing.
- Daily standups
- Block calendars
- Remove distractions

### Scope Hammering

**The Conversation:**
1. What's absolutely required for launch?
2. What's nice to have but not required?
3. What would we love but can cut?
4. What can we fix after launch?

**Rules:**
- Nice-to-haves get cut first
- Launch with less, iterate after
- Better done than perfect
- Set a date, hold the date

## 10.10 Presenting to Executives

### The Executive Mindset

**Understand Their Constraints:**
- Many topics in a day
- Limited time per topic
- Need to make decisions
- Care about outcomes, not details

### The Executive Briefing Format

**1. Start With the Ask**
"I need X from you" or "I'm here to inform you of Y."

Put this in the first 30 seconds.

**2. Context (Brief)**
- What's the situation?
- Why does it matter?
- What are the constraints?

3-5 sentences max.

**3. Options (If Decision Needed)**
- Option A: Pros/Cons
- Option B: Pros/Cons
- My recommendation: X because Y

**4. Backup Detail**
Have slides ready for questions, but don't present them unless asked.

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Instead |
|---------|--------------|---------|
| Starting with background | They lose patience | Lead with the point |
| Too much detail | They don't need it | Summarize ruthlessly |
| Burying the ask | They miss it | State it clearly, early |
| No recommendation | Forces them to decide without context | Have a point of view |
| Defensiveness | Signals insecurity | Own issues, show solutions |

### Know Your Numbers

**Always Be Able to Answer:**
- What's this costing?
- What's the timeline?
- What are the risks?
- What's the impact if we don't do this?

If you don't know, say "I'll follow up" - don't guess.

## 10.11 Career Narratives

### Tell Your Own Story

**The Insight:** Your career is a story. You're the author. Make it coherent.

**Components:**
- Where you came from
- What drives you
- Where you're going
- What makes you unique

### Building Your Narrative

**Exercise:**
1. What are the 3-5 major phases of your career?
2. What connects them? (Theme)
3. What did you learn in each?
4. What's the next chapter?

**Example Narrative:**
"I started as an IC focused on distributed systems. I moved into management because I saw more leverage in growing teams than writing code. Now I'm building engineering organizations that ship reliable systems at scale."

### Career Growth Strategies

**Growth at One Company:**
- Deep context and relationships
- Seen progress over time
- Trust built
- Risk: stagnation, single perspective

**Hopping Between Companies:**
- Breadth of experience
- Higher title/comp jumps
- Multiple perspectives
- Risk: seen as flight risk, never deep

**Hybrid Approach:**
- 3-5 years per company
- Leave when growth stalls
- Stay long enough to show impact

### Staff+ Career Ladders

**The Split:**
At Staff level and above, the path splits:
- Management track: Director → VP → CTO
- IC track: Staff → Principal → Distinguished

**Both Are Valid:**
- Management: Leverage through people
- Staff+: Leverage through technical influence

**Choose Based On:**
- What energizes you
- Where your skills are strongest
- What the organization needs
